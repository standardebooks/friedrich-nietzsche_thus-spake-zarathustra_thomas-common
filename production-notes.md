Quotation marks usage in the original text is idiosyncratic: some quotes aren’t closed for several paragraphs, and some spoken parts do not use quotation marks at all. For the former, open marks were added in an editorial commit to make it easier to follow that the same speech is still ongoing; latter cases were left untouched.

Typogrify:

The text features several two-em-dashes and a three-em-dash at seemingly random places. This is a deliberate attempt by Nietzsche, preserved in the translation, to add rhythm and musicality to the text. Typogrify tries to change them back to regular em-dashes and/or add word joiners after them.

Chapter 74, line 102: A comma followed by an em-dash, also intended by the author, is targetted for removal.

Several instances of “Ye‑a” through the text use a non-breaking hyphen to stretch out the word. Typogrify incorrectly attempts to replace them with a regular hyphen.

Modernize-spelling:

Chapter 37, line 27: “moon-eyes” keeps the hyphen because it refers to eyes with moon-like quality and not a type of fish.

Chapter 40: “fire-dog” refers to a fantastical creature and not part of a fireplace and thus keeps the hyphen.

Chapter 50: “white-head” refers to a white-haired head, not a skin problem.
